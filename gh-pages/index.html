Biography

Emigration to the United States from Czarist Russia during World War I had crucial bearing on Zena's life. At four years old, she made the long journey with her mother and sister from the Ukraine across Asia to Japan to Seattle, ultimately reuniting with her father in Philadelphia. That experience and the ensuing adjustment to urban America engendered an empathy for common people and their struggles. The early recognition of her artistic talent by public grade school teachers gave Zena an advantage in education and the confident start to her eventual career as an artist and teacher. After her graduation from high school in 1928 the year and a half's study at the Moore Institute, a design and arts college for women, she became convinced to study the fine arts rather than take the path toward commercial art.

Zena would stay true to the idealism of her youth. As the Great Depression brought devastating unemployment and poverty, Zena became active in the labor movement. She took factory work to help pay for art supplies and studied informally at the Graphic Sketch Club and in the Art Department of Philadelphia's Settlement Music School.

Collections

Zena executed numerous portrait commissions, primarily in sculpture, but also in drawing and painting, such as those of S. Jay Levey, whose wife founded the National Parkinson Foundation in tribute to him; Miami Beach Mayor Chuck Hall; and Rabbi Irving Lehrman. In addition to public and private commissions, she sought out ordinary people whose inner beauty she saw and highly accomplished people like Scott Nearing and Linus Pauling for her subjects. Other portrait busts in her studio archive include those of John F. Kennedy, Richard Nixon, Martin Luther King, Jr., and Mahatma Gandhi.

Her portrait of Scott Nearing — political science professor, economist, radical, and founder of the modern ecology movement — is in the permanent collection of the National Portrait Gallery, Smithsonian Institution, Washington, D.C. Zena’s work is represented in other public art collections: The Wolfsonian and The Jewish Museum of Florida, Miami Beach; The Fellowship, Pennsylvania Academy of the Fine Arts, Philadelphia; and the Washington County Museum of Fine Arts, Hagerstown, Maryland.

Her Artists Speak for Peace archive belongs to the Swarthmore College Peace Collection, Swarthmore, PA.
